The Secret to a Perfect Sourdough Crust

M
Maria DupontHead Baker & Co-founder

After fifteen years and more loaves than we could ever count, we've developed an almost obsessive relationship with the crust. Not just any crust ‚Äî the kind that shatters when you slice it, that crackles as it cools on the rack, that makes the whole room smell of something ancient and good.

The secret, as with most things in bread, is not one thing. It's the combination of five or six small decisions that compound into something remarkable.

1. Start with the right fermentation

A crust gets its flavour from the crumb, not the other way around. If your fermentation is rushed or too warm, the acids that develop ‚Äî lactic and acetic ‚Äî won't have time to fully express themselves. We do a cold retard overnight in the fridge at 4¬∞C. The dough develops slowly, the gluten relaxes, and by morning you have a loaf ready to form and bake.

2. Steam in the first 20 minutes

This is the single most impactful thing you can do at home. Steam keeps the surface of the dough moist during oven spring, allowing the loaf to expand fully before the crust sets. We use a Dutch oven ‚Äî lid on for the first 20 minutes, lid off for the last 25. The result is a blistered, mahogany crust that you simply can't achieve without it.

3. Let it cool completely

We know. It's torture. But cutting into a loaf straight from the oven compresses the crumb and releases steam that should be working to finish the crust. Give it at least 45 minutes. An hour is better. The crust will crackle as it cools ‚Äî that sound is the moisture escaping and the crust setting properly. Listen for it.

There's more ‚Äî scoring technique, oven temperature, hydration levels ‚Äî but master these three and you'll already be baking bread that surprises people. That's the whole point, really.

How to Make Butter Croissants at Home

A
Aisha KamaraPastry Chef

I'll be honest with you: croissants are a commitment. The lamination process ‚Äî folding cold butter into dough again and again to create those paper-thin layers ‚Äî takes two days and a cool kitchen. But when you pull a tray of perfectly golden, audibly crisp croissants from your own oven, it's one of the most satisfying things you can do in a kitchen.

The Ingredients

Everything starts with quality. Use European-style butter with at least 82% fat content ‚Äî the extra fat is what makes lamination work. Your flour should be strong bread flour (around 12% protein) to handle the gluten development during folding. And your starter (or commercial yeast) should be fresh and active.

Day 1: The D√©trempe

Mix your dough just until combined ‚Äî you don't want full gluten development yet, because you'll be working the dough a lot more during lamination. Shape it into a rectangle, wrap it tightly, and refrigerate overnight. The cold rest is not optional.

Day 2: Lamination

Beat your cold butter into a thin, pliable sheet ‚Äî the same temperature as the dough. Lock it into the dough and fold: three letter folds with 30-minute rests in the fridge between each. After your final fold, roll out and cut into triangles. Roll each one from base to tip, curving the ends inward. Prove at cool room temperature (18‚Äì20¬∞C) until visibly puffy and slightly wobbly when you shake the tray. Then bake at 200¬∞C with steam. Perfection awaits.

Why We Only Use Heritage Grain Flour

T
Thomas BrunCo-founder & Operations

In 2011, two years into running BakeryStore, I drove two hours north to visit a small mill that had been operating continuously since 1847. I expected to find a curiosity. What I found changed how we bake.

The miller, a quietly passionate man in his sixties, pulled open a paper sack and handed me a pinch of Einkorn flour. I pressed it between my fingers. It was warm, slightly sweet-smelling, almost nutty. Nothing like the bleached commodity flour we'd been using. I asked him what the difference was.

What "Heritage" Actually Means

Heritage or ancient grains ‚Äî Einkorn, Emmer, Spelt, Rouge de Bordeaux ‚Äî are wheat varieties that have been grown largely unchanged for hundreds or even thousands of years. They were never bred for high yield or shelf stability. They were bred for flavour, nutrition, and adaptability to their specific soil and climate.

Modern commercial wheat, by contrast, has been selected almost entirely for yield and gluten content. The result is flour that performs predictably at industrial scale, but lacks the complexity of older varieties. When you eat supermarket bread, you're often eating the crop designed for efficiency, not taste.

What It Means for Our Bread

Heritage flour has a lower gluten content than modern wheat, which means our doughs are more delicate. They require longer fermentation, gentler handling, and more attention. Our bakers have had to re-learn how to read a loaf. But the payoff is a bread with genuine depth ‚Äî layers of nuttiness, natural sweetness, and an open crumb that you simply don't get from commodity flour.

We pay more for it. We always will. Good bread costs more to make than people expect, and we think that's a story worth telling.
